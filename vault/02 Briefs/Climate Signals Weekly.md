# Climate Signals Weekly

> Synthesised from the week's L1 source notes under `../01 Sources/`. The `azimuth-curator`
> fleet role evolves this single note in place each week. azimuth reports **observed**
> climate measurements — recorded atmospheric concentrations, recorded sea-ice extent, and
> recorded regional temperature/precipitation anomalies — never a forecast of what the climate
> will do. Every claim links to the L1 note it rests on. This brief reads three independent
> instrument channels at once (atmospheric chemistry, the cryosphere, regional reanalysis) for
> the seven days to 2026-07-10, the synthesis a static single-source bundle cannot do.

## This week at a glance

- The cryosphere headline reversed: the NSIDC Arctic trend flag eased back from last week's
  **"record_low"** to **"below_average"** — recorded July extent of 8.53 million km² at a −1.65
  million km² anomaly, so the deficit actually *narrowed* (from −1.8) even as the absolute extent
  fell with the summer melt (from June's 9.1); atmospheric CO2 reads 429.97 ppm (~54% above the
  pre-industrial 280 ppm baseline) and 15 of the 25 monitored zones ran warm over the week
  ([[sea-ice-extent]], [[co2-monitoring]], [[climate-anomalies]]).
- The regional temperature field cooled sharply at the top: **no zone holds an `EXTREME` warm
  anomaly this week** (last week Europe sat at `EXTREME` +6.1 °C), and Europe itself eased right
  down to a mild +1.3 °C `NORMAL`; the warmest reading is now the Congo Basin at +3.8 °C
  (`MODERATE`) ([[climate-anomalies]]).
- The precipitation signal flipped from dry to wet: last cycle's lone dry zone (East Asia) is
  gone and two **wet** anomalies took the `EXTREME`/`MODERATE` flags instead — **Myanmar** at a
  +17 mm `EXTREME` wet departure (a monsoon signal) and **Latin America** at +6.4 mm `MODERATE`
  wet — while the **Western Antarctic Ice Sheet** carried the week's only `EXTREME` cold reading
  at −6.9 °C ([[climate-anomalies]]).

## Atmospheric chemistry (Mauna Loa)

- The NOAA Mauna Loa record reads a current 429.97 ppm CO2, 1.86 ppm above the year-ago
  428.11 ppm, with the feed's stated annual growth rate at 2.85 ppm/yr; the current daily reading
  eased ~0.5 ppm from last week's 430.48 and sits just below the June monthly average of
  431.44 ppm as the Northern-Hemisphere seasonal draw-down continues past the spring peak
  ([[co2-monitoring]]).
- The same station reads two other long-lived greenhouse gases this cycle: methane at 1937.86
  ppb (easing from 1940.46) and nitrous oxide at 339.76 ppb (flat) — recorded concentrations
  reported as-measured, with no projection attached ([[co2-monitoring]]).
- The 12-month trend in the feed now runs from 427.87 ppm (2025-07) through a seasonal dip to
  424.37 ppm (2025-09) and back up to the 432.34 ppm May peak, easing to 431.44 ppm in June —
  the saw-tooth seasonal cycle riding on the rising baseline that the +1.86 ppm year-on-year gap
  measures ([[co2-monitoring]]).

## Cryosphere & ocean (sea-ice channel)

- Arctic sea-ice extent is recorded at 8.53 million km² for 2026-07, an anomaly of −1.65
  million km² — and the feed's trend flag reads **"below_average"** this cycle, easing back one
  notch from the "record_low" it carried last week; the absolute extent fell from June's 9.1
  million km² with the summer melt, but the *anomaly* narrowed (from −1.8), and every one of the
  last twelve monthly readings in the feed still carries a negative anomaly — a twelve-month
  unbroken run below average ([[sea-ice-extent]]).
- The same ocean channel reports a global sea-surface-temperature anomaly of +0.61 °C (flat),
  mean sea level 98.7 mm above the 1993 reference (up from 98.1) at a recorded 4.4 mm/yr rise,
  and upper-ocean (0–700 m) heat content of 228.45 zettajoules — four recorded ocean
  measurements in one pull ([[sea-ice-extent]]).

## Regional anomaly map (25 zones)

- Of the 25 monitored zones for the week 2026-07-04 to 2026-07-10, 15 were flagged warm, 8 cold
  and 2 wet — and the top of the temperature field eased markedly: **no zone reached `EXTREME`
  warmth** (last week Europe held `EXTREME` at +6.1 °C), with the Congo Basin now the warmest at
  +3.8 °C (`MODERATE`), ahead of the Tibetan Plateau (+2.8 °C), the Sahel (+2.5 °C) and Greenland
  (+2.3 °C) ([[climate-anomalies]]).
- The precipitation story flipped from dry to wet: last week's lone dry zone (East Asia, −7.0)
  is gone, and the two flagged precip anomalies this cycle are both wet — Myanmar at an `EXTREME`
  +17 mm departure (a monsoon signal) and Latin America at a `MODERATE` +6.4 mm — while East Asia
  itself reads a mild +0.5 °C warm ([[climate-anomalies]]).
- The Western Antarctic Ice Sheet swung hard to the cold side, taking the week's only `EXTREME`
  temperature flag at −6.9 °C (from a −0.8 °C reading last cycle) as the Antarctic winter
  deepened; the coldest non-polar zones are Ukraine (−2.3 °C), Australia (−1.9 °C) and Southern
  Africa (−1.4 °C) ([[climate-anomalies]]).

## Reading the week

- Read together, the three channels describe a recorded picture that eased at almost every top
  reading this cycle: a CO2 concentration still ~54% above pre-industrial but ticking down ~0.5
  ppm on the seasonal draw-down, an Arctic sea-ice flag that stepped back from "record_low" to
  "below_average" (the anomaly narrowing to −1.65 even as extent fell seasonally), and a regional
  map that stayed warm-leaning (15 of 25) but lost every `EXTREME` warm anomaly. azimuth reports
  what the instruments measured — the value, the anomaly sign, the severity flag — and stops
  there; the editorial line keeps the brief to observed facts and excludes any projection of what
  follows ([[co2-monitoring]], [[sea-ice-extent]], [[climate-anomalies]]).
- The synthesis value this cycle is the cross-channel join a single feed cannot make: the poles
  registered with sharply opposite signs — the Arctic pairs a below-average recorded ice extent
  with a +1.3 °C warm-air anomaly over the same region (low ice still co-located with warm air),
  while the Antarctic pole took the week's only `EXTREME` reading at −6.9 °C cold as its winter
  deepened. The bipolar contrast widened rather than the warm-in-tandem picture of earlier cycles
  ([[sea-ice-extent]], [[climate-anomalies]]).
